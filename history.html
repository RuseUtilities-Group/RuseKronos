Soviet Involvement in Afghanistan

Introduction

On December 24, 1979, the Soviet Union invades Afghanistan, under the pretext of upholding the Soviet-Afghan Friendship Treaty of 1978.

As midnight approached, the Soviets organized a massive military airlift into Kabul, involving an estimated 280 transport aircraft and three divisions of almost 8,500 men each.

Main people/leaders involved

Leonid Brezhnev - General Secretary of the USSR


Yuri Andropov - Second Secretary of the USSR


Dmitriy Ustinov - Marshal of the Soviet Union


Babrak Karmal - General Secretary of the People's Democratic Party of Afghanistan


Abdul Rashid Dostum - Marshal in the Afghan National Army


Aims of the main people involved

  • Plan to support the Communist Afghan government against the anti-communist Muslim gurreilla forces.
  • Devout Muslims and largely anti-communist population resented the extensive land and social reforms imposed the the new Communist government
  • Aim of the Soviets was to prop up their new client state under the leadership of Babrak Kamal

Historical significance of the event within the context of the Cold War

The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan had a profound long-term impact in the context of the Cold War. The Soviet Union was significantly weakened and never recovered from the public relations and financial losses of their failed invasion. The Soviet army was more accustomed to large scale, conventional forms of warfare such as that of World War II, but the conflict in Afghanistan was completely different. Soviet soldiers of the Red Army were also unable to engage smaller groups of Mujahideen fighters, severely weakening the combat potential of the Soviet Union. The losses in Afghanistan significantly contributed toward the dissolution of the whole USSR in 1991. 

The victories in Afghanistan paved the way for modern terrorist groups, such as the Taliban, Al Qaeda and ISIS in the Middle East. These groups have had a colossal effect on the rest of the world, often performing atrocities of murder, such as the September 11 attacks and the Charlie Hebdo shooting. These terrorist groups were also involved in conflicts against the United States, such as the later War in Afghanistan and the War on Terror.
